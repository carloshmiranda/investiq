## What Is Dividend Investing?

Dividend investing is a strategy where you build a portfolio of stocks, ETFs, or funds that regularly pay out a portion of their earnings to shareholders. Instead of relying solely on price appreciation, you earn income simply by holding these assets.

A **dividend** is a cash payment made by a company to its shareholders, typically on a quarterly basis. For example, if you own 100 shares of a stock paying €1.00 per share annually, you receive €100 in dividends each year — without selling a single share.

## Why Dividends Matter for Long-Term Investors

Dividends do more than just supplement your income. They are a powerful signal of financial health:

- **Consistent dividends** indicate a company generates reliable cash flow
- **Dividend growth** compounds your returns over time without additional investment
- **Reinvested dividends** (DRIP) accelerate wealth accumulation through compound growth

Historically, dividends have accounted for a significant portion of total stock market returns. Research from Hartford Funds shows that from 1930 to 2022, dividends contributed approximately **40% of the total return** of the S&P 500.

## Key Metrics to Evaluate Dividend Stocks

Before investing, you should understand a few critical numbers:

### Dividend Yield

The yield is simply the annual dividend divided by the current share price:

```
Yield = Annual Dividend per Share ÷ Share Price × 100
```

A 3% yield means that for every €1,000 invested, you earn €30 per year in dividends. Be cautious of unusually high yields (above 8–10%) — they often signal a struggling company whose stock price has dropped sharply.

### Payout Ratio

The payout ratio tells you what fraction of earnings a company pays as dividends:

```
Payout Ratio = Dividends per Share ÷ Earnings per Share × 100
```

A ratio below 60–70% is generally sustainable. Very high payout ratios (above 90%) may indicate the dividend is at risk of being cut.

### Dividend Growth Rate

Look for companies that have consistently raised their dividend over many years. These "dividend growers" tend to be high-quality businesses with competitive moats.

## Building a Diversified Dividend Portfolio

A good dividend portfolio isn't just a collection of high-yield stocks. Diversification across sectors helps reduce risk:

- **Consumer staples** (e.g., Unilever, Procter & Gamble) — stable demand regardless of economic conditions
- **Utilities** — regulated revenues and long-term contracts support reliable payouts
- **Real estate investment trusts (REITs)** — legally required to distribute 90%+ of taxable income
- **Healthcare** — aging demographics provide long-term demand tailwinds
- **International ETFs** — exposure to European and global dividend payers (e.g., VHYL, VGWD)

## The DRIP Effect: Reinvesting for Compound Growth

A **Dividend Reinvestment Plan (DRIP)** automatically uses your dividend payments to purchase additional shares. Over time, this creates a powerful compounding effect:

If you invest €10,000 in a fund yielding 4% annually and reinvest all dividends, here's how it grows over 20 years (assuming 2% dividend growth per year):

| Year | Portfolio Value | Annual Dividends |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| 5    | €12,400        | €498            |
| 10   | €15,300        | €665            |
| 20   | €23,200        | €1,088          |

This is the core mechanic behind the phrase "make your money work for you."
